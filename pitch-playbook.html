Internal Playbook

The Pitch Playbook

Three ways to present. Three stories to tell. Three styles to choose from. Pick one from each — or mix.

Demo Day: March 23, 2026 · Team Elktron · "More. Better. Faster."
Part One

Three Ways to Deliver

How the project reaches the judges. Each format has different strengths. Pick the one that matches your hardware readiness on March 22.

1 Recommended

Pre-Recorded Video + Live Q&A

2:30 edited video shown to judges, then you answer questions live with the robots on the table.

  • Why this wins: You control the narrative. No live-demo failures. Judges see polished footage AND real hardware.
  • Film: 1 evening (March 21 or 22)
  • Edit: 3-4 hours (Talha)
  • Format: MP4, 1080p, subtitles burned in
  • Live element: Robots on table, powered on. Judges can see/touch them during Q&A.
Best for Hardware works but is finicky. Story matters more than live proof.
2 High Risk

Live Demo on the Floor

2-3 minute live walkthrough with both robots running in real-time. Romeo narrates, bots perform.

  • Why this wins: Nothing beats a live robot moving on the DC floor. Instant People's Choice.
  • Risk: Motors stall. Camera loses lock. Arm drops the optic. Wi-Fi interference.
  • Mitigation: Pre-record a backup video. If live fails, pivot: "Here's what it looked like in testing" and play the tape.
  • Need: Floor access approval, extension cord, clear aisle, vendor volunteer (Raphael).
Best for Everything works perfectly in testing. You've done 5+ dry runs.
3 Safe Fallback

Slide Deck + Video Clips

5 slides max with embedded 15-30s video clips of each robot. Romeo presents, Talha runs slides.

  • Slide 1: The problem — one photo of a tech escorting a vendor
  • Slide 2: Escort bot clip (15s) + one sentence
  • Slide 3: SO-101 arm clip (15s) + one sentence
  • Slide 4: Dashboard screenshot + impact numbers
  • Slide 5: "More. Better. Faster." + team photo
Best for Arm kit arrived late. Only escort bot works. Need a polished Plan B.
Part Two

Three Stories to Tell

Same robots, three different narrative angles. Pick the one that fits your energy and what the judges seem to care about.

1 Strongest

"I Do This Job Every Day"

The angle: Romeo is a DCT. He built robots to solve his own pain. This isn't a software engineer imagining DC problems — it's a technician who's tired of escorting vendors and seating optics by hand.

Opening line: "I'm a data center technician at EVI01. I seat optics, escort vendors, and inspect racks. I built two robots to do it for me."

  • Emotional hook: Authenticity. A builder solving their own problem.
  • Key moment: Show Romeo demonstrating the task to the arm — teaching it by hand, then stepping back.
  • Closing line: "The best automation comes from the people who do the work."
Power Judges remember the person, not just the product.
2 Scale Play

"What If Every DC Had These?"

The angle: Start with the math. X optics per week, Y vendor escorts, Z tech-hours burned. Then show how robots collapse those numbers.

Opening line: "CoreWeave is building data centers faster than anyone. The floor work doesn't scale with headcount — it scales with robots."

  • Emotional hook: Ambition. This isn't a toy — it's the start of a fleet.
  • Key moment: Dashboard showing both robots running simultaneously.
  • Closing line: "Two robots today. Twenty next quarter. Same software."
Power Best for OKR alignment and exec-level judges.
3 Crowd Favorite

"We Built This in a Week"

The angle: Show the build journey. Parts arriving. Chassis assembly. First motor test. First person detection. Montage energy.

Opening line: "Seven days ago this was a $25 chassis kit and a Raspberry Pi on a desk."

  • Emotional hook: Transformation. The audience watches something become real.
  • Key moment: Timelapse of assembly, then cut to the bot rolling and following.
  • Closing line: "From parts to patrol in one week. That's building with velocity."
Power Best for People's Choice. Everyone loves a build montage.

Mix & Match

You can combine stories. Start with the DCT story (30s), show the build montage (30s), end with the scale vision (30s). The strongest pitches layer personal + proof + vision.

Part Three

Three Visual Styles

How the video and slides look and feel. This sets the tone before anyone says a word.

1 Recommended

The Floor Report

Documentary. Steady handheld footage. Natural lighting. Real DC floor sounds. Minimal graphics — just white text on footage.

  • Music: Low electronic hum, almost ambient. Or no music — just floor sounds.
  • Text: White sans-serif, bottom-left. No animations.
  • Color grade: Slightly desaturated, cool tones. Industrial.
  • Transitions: Hard cuts only. No fades, no wipes.
  • Vibe: "This is real. This is happening."
Pair with The DCT Story or The Build Story
2 Hard to Edit

The Launch Trailer

Hype reel. Fast cuts synced to music. Bold title cards. Motion graphics for stats. Dramatic reveals.

  • Music: Building electronic track with a drop. Drums hit when the robot moves.
  • Text: Large, bold, animated. Numbers count up. Stats fly in.
  • Color grade: High contrast, punchy.
  • Transitions: Fast cuts on beat. Speed ramps.
  • Vibe: "The future is here and we built it this week."
Pair with The Scale Story or The Build Story
3 Safest

The Investor Deck

Clean minimal. White backgrounds. One idea per frame. Robot footage in clean boxes. Calm. Confident. Precise.

  • Music: Light piano or no music. VO carries it.
  • Text: Dark text on white. Big numbers.
  • Color grade: Clean, bright, slightly warm whites.
  • Transitions: Smooth slides. Fade to white.
  • Vibe: "We're professionals who built something serious."
Pair with The Scale Story. Best if arm footage is limited.
Factor Documentary Hype Reel Clean Minimal
Footage needed Most — long, steady takes Medium — many short clips Least — a few key clips
Edit difficulty Easy — cut and arrange Hard — timing, effects, sync Medium — clean layouts
Talha skill match Good — straightforward cuts Harder — needs rhythm sense Good — template-friendly
If robots fail Can still work with B-roll Falls flat without footage Slides carry it
Crowd response Respect Excitement Trust

Suggested Combo

Documentary style + DCT story + pre-recorded video with live Q&A. Lowest risk, highest authenticity. Talha can edit it. The robots sell themselves when they move — no flashy effects needed.

Pass-around prop

A small switch with empty SFP ports. The arm demo target. Even unpowered, it makes the task real.

Demo surface

Powered on, camera light active. Even idle, a robot with a blinking camera is more interesting than any slide.

Attention magnet

SO-101 clamped to table edge. If trained, do a quick teleop demo. If not, it's still a real robot arm.

Visual anchor

Interactive handoff

Part Five

Tools for Filming & Editing

Everything Talha needs to produce the video. All free or already available.

DaVinci Resolve

Free — macOS / Windows

Professional video editor. Color grading, cuts, text overlays, audio sync. This is the one to use.

CapCut Desktop

Free — easier option

Simpler editor. Auto-captions, templates, beat sync. If Resolve feels overwhelming, start here.

Phone Camera

Free — already have

Film in 1080p 30fps. Lock exposure. Use a tripod or prop against books. Steady > high-res.

OBS Studio

Free — screen recording

Record the dashboard demo. Set to 1080p, capture browser window.

Canva

Free tier

Title cards, posters, badges, stickers, slide deck. Drag-and-drop. Talha-friendly.

Epidemic Sound / Artlist

Free trial

Search "tech documentary minimal." Download before trial ends. One track is all you need.

Voice Memo (iPhone)

Free — voiceover

Record VO in a quiet room. Phone 6 inches from mouth. Clarity > quality for 2:30.

Google Slides

Free — fallback deck

Embed video clips into slides. Present from laptop. Works if going with Method 3.

Part Six

Talha's Playbook

Step-by-step for the media lead. No jargon. Do these in order.

The Only Thing That Matters

You are not making a movie. You are making a 2 minute and 30 second video that shows two robots doing real work. The judges will watch it once. They need to understand it immediately. Simple beats fancy every time.

Week Before Demo (March 16-21)

1
Download DaVinci Resolve (free) or CapCut (free, easier). Install it. Open it. Make a blank project called "Elktron." That's it for day one.
2
Watch one YouTube tutorial: search "DaVinci Resolve beginner 2024." Watch the first 15 minutes. You now know enough. Build momentum by doing, not watching.
3
Practice edit: Film yourself walking for 30 seconds. Import it. Cut it to 10 seconds. Add white text that says "TEST." Export. If you can do this, you can edit the demo video.
4
Make title cards in Canva. You need 3: "2 tasks. 2 robots. 1 crew." / "Before: [X]. After: [Y]." / "More. Better. Faster." White text on black. Save as PNG.

Filming Day (March 21 or 22)

5
Film everything on the shot list. Romeo tells you what to point the camera at. Your job: hold steady, keep the robot in frame, film each shot 3 times. Film MORE than you need.
6
Record Romeo's voiceover. Quiet room. Romeo reads the script into a phone. One take — if he messes up, pause and re-say that line. Cut mistakes later.
7
Screen-record the dashboard. Open OBS, hit record, click through the dashboard for 30 seconds. Stop. Done.

Edit Day (March 22)

8
Import everything into your project: footage clips, voiceover audio, title card PNGs, dashboard recording, music track.
9
Lay the voiceover first. This is your backbone. When Romeo talks about the escort bot — put escort bot footage there. When he talks about the arm — arm footage there.
10
Drop title cards between sections. 2-3 seconds each. Black screen, white text. They give the audience a breath.
11
Add music LAST, keep it at 10-15% volume. If you can't hear Romeo clearly over the music, it's too loud. Music is wallpaper.
12
Export: 1080p, MP4. Watch once. Send to Romeo. Fix flags. Export again. Done.

Talha's Golden Rules

Rule 1: Stuck for 10 minutes? Ask Romeo or YouTube. Don't spiral.
Rule 2: Ugly and done beats pretty and unfinished. Ship it.
Rule 3: The voiceover IS the video. Everything else is decoration.
Rule 4: Film everything 3 times. Edit is just picking the best take.
Rule 5: The whole video is 2:30. That's one song. You can do this.

Part Seven

Tips & Tricks

Hard-won hackathon presentation wisdom.

Filming Tips

  • Film in landscape (horizontal). Always. No vertical video.
  • Lock exposure — tap and hold on the subject until AE/AF Lock shows.
  • Use a stack of books as a tripod. Steady shot > handheld.
  • Film robots at their level — get low. Eye-level makes it look alive.
  • Get one "hero shot" of each robot from a low angle. This is your thumbnail.
  • Film DC floor B-roll — lights, cable trays, racks. 5 seconds. Use for transitions.
  • Film the team working — soldering, typing, assembling. Build montage gold.
  • Turn off notifications on your phone before filming.

Presentation Tips

  • First 10 seconds decide everything. Start with the problem or a robot moving.
  • Say "More. Better. Faster." at least once. Judges listen for their theme.
  • Mention the team is cross-functional — DC ops, software, hardware, media.
  • Don't know an answer? "Great phase-2 question. Here's what we'd explore..."
  • Let judges touch the robots during Q&A. Physical = memorable.
  • Have the dashboard running on a second screen. Casually point to it.
  • Don't apologize for what isn't done. Frame: "Phase 1 is X. Phase 2 is Y."
  • End with a clear sentence. "Elktron. More hands on the floor."

The 3-Second Rule

No shot should last longer than 5 seconds without a cut, text, or new element. Attention spans at demo day are brutal. Keep it moving.

The Buddy Test

Show the video to someone who knows nothing about the project. If they can't explain what Elktron does in one sentence, the video isn't clear enough.

Audio = 70% of Video Quality

Perfect audio + mediocre footage = professional. Perfect footage + bad audio = amateur. Prioritize clear voiceover over fancy shots. Record in a closet if needed — fabric absorbs echo.
