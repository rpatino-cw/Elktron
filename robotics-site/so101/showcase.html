Problem Statement

Why this exists

DCTs spend a significant portion of each shift on repetitive, fine-motor tasks that follow the same pattern every time: seating optics into transceiver cages, visually inspecting rack fronts for unseated cables or LED faults, routing and dressing cables along rack rails.

These tasks are physically draining, error-prone at volume, and don't require human judgment — just consistent, repeatable motion. A low-cost robot arm trained to replicate these motions could free DCTs to focus on diagnostic and break-fix work that actually requires thinking.

Target DC tasks

1. Optic Seating

Best demo target

Pick an SFP/QSFP transceiver from a tray, align it to the cage, and push it in until it clicks. Small workspace, repeatable motion, clear success/fail.

2. Rack Inspection

CV-heavy, less manipulation

Arm-mounted camera sweeps across the rack front. CV detects unseated cables, missing drives, LED fault patterns.

3. Cable Dressing

Hardest — needs 200+ demos

Route a patch cable along the rack rail and secure it. Requires complex grasping and path planning.

Hackathon strategy: Demo optic seating only. It's the most tractable task — small workspace, repeatable motion, 50 demonstrations is enough to train a working policy.

How It Works

Leader-follower imitation learning

The SO-101 uses a leader-follower architecture. Two identical arms: the leader (lighter servos, manually moved) and the follower (stronger servos, mirrors the leader). When you move the leader arm, the follower copies every joint position in real time while recording the full trajectory + camera frames.

This recorded data becomes the training set. The LeRobot framework (HuggingFace) trains an ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) policy that learns to predict the next sequence of joint positions given the current camera frame and joint state.

Phase 1
Record
Move the leader arm through the task. Follower mirrors + records joints + camera.
python record.py --task optic_seating
Phase 2
Train
ACT policy learns the motion from 50 demonstrations. GPU required.
python train.py --task optic_seating
Phase 3
Deploy
Follower arm executes the task autonomously using the trained model.
python deploy.py --task optic_seating

What is ACT?

Action Chunking with Transformers is an imitation learning policy from Tony Zhao et al. (Stanford). Instead of predicting one action at a time, it predicts a "chunk" of future actions (e.g., the next 100 timesteps) all at once, then executes them with temporal ensembling. This makes movements smooth and coherent rather than jerky.

Why not reinforcement learning?

RL requires a reward function, a simulation, and thousands of episodes. Imitation learning requires a human to demonstrate the task ~50 times. For a hackathon, imitation learning is dramatically faster.

Alternative

Keyboard teleop (no leader arm)

If budget is tight, Haoran Xu demonstrated single-arm control via keyboard. Keys map to individual joints. Recording still works — just slower to demonstrate. Saves ~$135 on the leader arm.

Source: Haoran Xu — "Build & AI Hack the LeRobot SO-101" | Teleop branch
